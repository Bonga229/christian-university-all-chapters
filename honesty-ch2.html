Chapter 2: Honesty in Relationships

In nature, everything is connected — rivers to oceans, roots to trees, air to breath. Connection is survival. But for humans, connection often becomes the very place where trust is broken.

Why do friends turn into enemies? Why do sons and daughters grow distant from their parents? It’s rarely because of distance. It’s because somewhere along the way, honesty disappeared — with them, and with ourselves.

The first lie you ever told wasn’t to someone else. It was to yourself.

You said “I’m fine” when you weren’t. That small lie became a mask. The mask became a personality. And the personality became a prison.

My Second Year Story

In my second year of university, I had found my people. Three of us, doing everything together — eating, laughing, even failing tests side by side. We were stupid in the best way. But somewhere along the way, something changed.

We were all goofy, but they could stay goofy in front of anyone. I couldn’t. That part of me — the loud, hyper side — was something I’d started hating back in high school. I thought it made people take me less seriously, like I wasn’t worth listening to. I carried that into uni, pretending I didn’t care… but the truth was, it ate at me.

Being around them brought that side of me out again. Some people would call it ADHD. All I knew was: I wanted to change. I thought, “If I stay friends with them, I’ll never grow up.” Maybe that was true. Maybe it wasn’t. But I made my decision.

I didn’t tell them the real reason. Instead, I lied — said my parents didn’t want me doing this or that. Slowly, I ignored their calls, skipped meetups, acted busy. In my head, I was protecting myself. But really, I was just avoiding an uncomfortable conversation.

It ended badly. I lost two of the closest friends I’d ever had. Not because we disagreed. Not because we grew apart. But because I wasn’t honest from the start. I became the bad guy in my own story.

What Could Have Happened

If I had told them the truth — that I was struggling with who I was, that I wanted to work on myself — maybe they wouldn’t have understood at first. Maybe they would’ve been hurt. But at least they would’ve known. At least trust would still be there.

That’s the thing about honesty: it doesn’t guarantee people will stay. But it guarantees that if they leave, they leave knowing the real you — not the fake one you made to avoid conflict.

Why This Matters

We tell ourselves we’re protecting others by keeping quiet, but deep down, we’re protecting ourselves from rejection. Silence is not love. Lies are not kindness.

In Christian University, we choose to speak truth — with kindness, with love, but without fear. Because without honesty, there can be no real connection.
