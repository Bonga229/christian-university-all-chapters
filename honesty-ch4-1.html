Honesty – Chapter 4.1: Good vs Bad Choices

“If it works for you, then it is good.” That was the last drop of peer pressure that slipped out of my friend’s mouth. And honestly, it worked on me.

We had just received our salary. The mood was high, the temptation strong. My friend and I decided maybe it was time to go out and celebrate. And by celebrate, I mean drink. The thing is—I felt guilty. Deeply guilty. I had been on this on-and-off journey of self-improvement for a while. You know that stage: you start strong, you fall, you start again, you fall harder, you give up, then you come back. That cycle. At that moment, I was lost. A new job, new environment, and the habits I was trying to run from were chasing me down again.

So I told him, “I’m not too sure about this, man. I’ve been trying to quit alcohol.” That’s when he leaned back and hit me with a story.

He told me about a guy from his high school. A boy who drank so much he couldn’t cope without alcohol. At first it sounded like a tragedy. But then came the twist: the boy was actually doing better in school because of it. His grades were higher, his focus sharper—at least that’s what my friend said. And then came the punchline, the seed he wanted to plant in me: “See? For him, alcohol is good. If something works for you, then it is good.”

I laughed nervously. Because here’s the thing—alcohol never worked for me. It made me foggy, drained, regretful. But still, his words were the right amount of pressure at the wrong time. And I caved. We went out anyway. And as I swallowed each drink, I couldn’t silence the thought: is he right? Can something destructive really be called good, just because it helps in one area?

That’s when I began wrestling with the deeper question: what is good, and what is bad?

Good isn’t just what feels good in the moment. Good is harmony. It’s when your body, your mind, and your spirit align, and nothing inside you screams in protest after the fact. You can sleep at night without a war inside your chest. That is good. Bad, on the other hand, is betrayal—betrayal of yourself. You know it when you feel it: the weight in your stomach after a lie, the emptiness after chasing validation, the guilt that shadows a choice you knew wasn’t right.

Humans were built for good. You see it in how kindness heals, how truth cuts through confusion, how honesty lightens the soul. But the world confuses us. Manipulation whispers. Validation seduces. Fear chains us. Slowly, we start to blur the lines, calling bad “good” if it gives us results, and calling good “naïve” if it costs us something.

My friend believed his high school buddy proved that alcohol was good, because it “worked.” But working isn’t the same as being good. Poison can work. Lies can work. Manipulation can work. That doesn’t make them good. They may lift you up for a moment, but only at the cost of your foundation. And deep down, the guilt, the regret, the heaviness—it’s all proof that we know the difference, whether we admit it or not.

So maybe the truth is this: good strengthens, even if it hurts first. Bad weakens, even if it feels good first. And the more honest we are with ourselves, the harder it is to be manipulated into calling one the other.
