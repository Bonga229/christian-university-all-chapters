Honesty – Chapter 4.2: Intuition, the Voice Beneath the Noise

In the last chapter, we wrestled with the difference between good and bad. But here’s the real question: how do you even know which is which? How do you tell when your mind is tricking you, or when the world is feeding you lies dressed up as truth? That’s where intuition comes in.

Intuition is that quiet pull inside your chest, the gut feeling that whispers when your brain is too loud. It’s the voice that doesn’t argue with logic, it just says: “This isn’t right.” or “Go for it.” No essays, no evidence, no calculations. Just a raw, unfiltered sense. And most of the time, it’s right.

But here’s the problem—we’ve been taught to distrust it. From the time we were kids, we were told: “Don’t be silly, listen to your teacher. Don’t argue, do as you’re told. Don’t trust your feelings, they’ll mislead you.” Parents, schools, systems—they trained us to silence the one voice that was actually ours. Slowly, we learned to doubt ourselves. We learned to outsource our truth to authority, to the crowd, to trends. We stopped believing in our own compass.

And what happens when you ignore intuition? It rots. You feel it in your stomach—that ache of regret after you ignored the warning signs of a fake friend. The sick feeling when you didn’t listen to yourself about a job, a relationship, or even something as small as going out when you knew you should’ve stayed in. It feels like betrayal, because that’s exactly what it is—you betrayed yourself.

Your brain lies because it’s overprotective. It will come up with a thousand reasons why you shouldn’t try, why you should stay safe, why you should wait. Intuition isn’t like that. It isn’t scared. It isn’t manipulative. It’s the most honest signal you have because it doesn’t care about comfort—it cares about truth.

Trusting your intuition doesn’t mean you’ll never fail. It means when you do fail, you’ll know it was your choice. It was genuine. And strangely enough, that’s a win—because you didn’t abandon yourself. The more you listen, the sharper it gets. The more you silence it, the duller your sense of right and wrong becomes. And when that inner voice fades, you become easy to control, easy to mislead, easy to break.

So maybe the path to good isn’t always about logic, or even about rules. Maybe it’s about reconnecting with that honest voice within you. The one that says: “This is right. This is wrong. Trust me.”
