Honesty – Chapter 4: Honesty Is Freedom

When most people think about freedom, their minds jump straight to money. The freedom to buy what you want, travel anywhere, and live without limits. And yes, money can give you options. But money is not the only kind of freedom. In fact, it’s not even the most important kind.

There is another kind of freedom — the kind that nobody can give you, the kind you have to fight for within yourself. It’s the freedom to act without being enslaved by your impulses, fears, or emotions.

Think about this: what if freedom wasn’t just about buying a car or a house, but about going to bed at 9 p.m. even though your body screams to keep scrolling on your phone? What if freedom was walking up to the girl you’ve been staring at for weeks, even while your thoughts tell you you’re not good enough? What if freedom was admitting: “I want validation. I want attention. I want comfort.” And then making a choice knowing that at least you were true to yourself.

Most people think they’re free, but they’re not. They’re slaves to feelings they won’t even admit. They’ll say, “I don’t feel like working today.” They won’t admit the truth — that deep down, laziness feels safer than trying and failing. Or they’ll say, “I just like staying up late.” They won’t admit the truth — that distraction numbs them from facing what they’re running from.

This is where honesty comes in. You cannot be free until you admit what controls you. Because once you’re honest, you own it. And once you own it, you can choose what to do with it.

Here’s the part people miss: owning your emotions doesn’t mean suppressing them. It means acknowledging them without letting them lead you blindly. Say I’m furious at someone, so pissed off that all I can think about is knocking this guy down. If I’m not honest, I’ll say, “I’m fine, I don’t care.” But inside I’m boiling, and the anger leaks out in other ways. If I’m brutally honest, I’ll admit, “I want to smash this guy’s face. I want to hurt him.” That’s the real feeling. Now, I can actually decide: do I want to act on it, or do I want to let it pass? The point is, I’m not being mindlessly led by a temporary emotion.

That’s the real freedom. Not doing whatever you feel like in the moment — but knowing exactly what you feel, admitting it, and then acting with clarity instead of confusion.

So yes, money buys options. But honesty buys freedom. The kind of freedom nobody can take away, because it’s born inside you.
